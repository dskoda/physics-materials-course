## Course Description

Introduction to electrical, optical, and magnetic properties of solids. Free electron model, introduction to band theory and Schrödinger wave equation. Crystal bonding and lattice vibrations. Mechanisms and characterization of electrical conductivity, optical absorption, magnetic behavior, dielectrical properties, and p-n junctions.

## Learning Objectives

After this course, students will be able to:

- Describe the interplay between wave and particle behavior of electrons and their influence in explaining solid-state property of materials
- Interpret the postulates of quantum mechanics, solve simple cases of the Schrödinger's equation, and describe the solutions for the electron in the hydrogen atom.
- Explain how atoms and electrons behave in a lattice, including approximations such as the free electron model, phonons, and the origin of the band gap and effective masses of electrons/holes.
- Interpret electronic band structures, electronic density of states, and band diagrams for several junctions, including semiconductor materials.
- Quantitatively describe the operation mechanisms of semiconductor devices, from P-N junctions to MOSFETs, and what is the physical origin of their behavior.
- Quantitatively describe the physical origins of optical, dielectric, and magnetic properties of materials.

**Readings** correspond to chapters/sections of the textbook, L. Solymar, D. Walsh, R.R.A. Syms. Electrical Properties of Materials. 9th ed. Oxford University Press. 2014

**Videos** are interesting videos related to each of the lectures.
The videos are educational, extra content that allows one to explore different perspectives on each lecture.
Videos (and sponsored ads) do not necessarily represent the views of the instructor or the University.

**Demos** are interactive visualizations created by the instructor to illustrate each of the physical phenomena and equations.
The visualizations help gaining intuition on each of the phenomena beyond memorizing equations.
In some cases, animations appeal to your intuition in known concepts (e.g., heat or sound) to explain harder concepts in quantum mechanics (e.g., electron wavefunction).
